Season 2: From Fragments to the Whole / 
Exhibition Caption:


Ano Bldg: From Fragments to the Whole

This exhibition presents part of the design process for Ano Bldg, a mixed-use building proposed for a hypothetical site facing Yokoyama-cho Odori, through models and drawings.
The exhibition is composed of two periods, and the current period is Period 2: “From Fragments to the Whole”.

Period 1: “In Search of Abstraction”
We can grasp the overall image of the city only through the accumulation of fragmentary perceptions.
In Period 1: “In Search of Abstraction”, we began from our own way of seeing the city, and reconsidered the unavoidable act of abstraction in the process of creating architecture by reconstructing façades from fragmented relationships.

By quoting fragments from the surrounding buildings and composing façades as if weaving them together, we attempted to create architecture as a concrete form of abstraction, in which existing architectural languages that appear singular can be interpreted more complexly through the layering of diverse meanings.

Period 2: “From Fragments to the Whole”
In this second period, we further explore the abstract quality of Ano Bldg.

The façades, made by weaving in elements from the surroundings, each contain different orders and compositional logics drawn from neighboring buildings. When the façade is viewed as a whole, if it is integrated only into the language of the surrounding buildings, its abstract quality is lost. Yet if it is separated too strongly into an autonomous language, the network of relationships does not emerge.
It is precisely the interstice within this in-between state that barely allows architecture as abstraction to stand. Abstraction is not a mere gathering of elements; it is formed by the existence of interstices that both connect and separate fragments.
To begin with, there are always ambiguous gaps in the process through which we perceive the world. For example, human perception constructs an overall image by unconsciously filling in the gaps between fragments. However, the edges of that image are not always clear. The interstice is what holds fragments together, while at the same time making their relationships ambiguous.
In the perception of architecture as well, there are parts that, no matter how hard we try, we can neither enter nor fully perceive. For example, the full form of a column hidden behind a wall, or the depth of the space between the floor above and the ceiling below. Such invisible parts can also be understood as interstices.

We became aware of the existence of these interstices during the first exhibition period. The exploration of abstraction does not end. The proposal for Ano Bldg is still in the midst of study, and this exhibition itself, across both the first and second periods, is also part of that process.

Exhibiting the Exhibition Space
In the venue, wires corresponding to the full width of the exhibition building are stretched as if floating through the space. The sagging of these wires suggests invisible interstices that are difficult to grasp visually, such as the shape of columns, the thickness of walls, and the depth of the space between ceiling and floor.
It is an attempt to reconstruct, within the space, the relationships between fragments of the actual exhibition venue. It is also a device that guides the viewer’s gaze toward the invisible interstices that exist between fragments.
These wires are intended to subtly alter the gaze and movement of visitors, and to unsettle the way the space itself is perceived.
They are not merely structural elements, but a process of architectural thought that attempts to bring a whole into being from fragments.
