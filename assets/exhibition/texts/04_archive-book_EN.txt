Ano Bldg Archive Book / Notes 1: An Exploration of Perception / Opening:


The world seems already to be there before we see it.
And yet, the way that world appears to us changes greatly depending on the distance from which we approach it, and on the gestures with which we try to touch it.

For example, a person working on the fifth floor of a building may come to know the city from a height different from that of the ground, looking down at the road through a window. A person cleaning an apartment building may remember, through the body, the landings of the shared stairway or the positions of small windows, and may come to know the presence of the building across the way through those gaps. Even within the same city, not everyone is seeing the same landscape. When one’s position changes, the contours that come into view, and the details that draw one’s attention, naturally change as well.
The image of the city is not singular to begin with. Even within the same Yokoyama-cho, a person looking down from a window, a person cleaning the stairs, and a person carrying goods will each remember different heights, smells, and details that catch their attention.

When I first visited Nihonbashi Yokoyama-cho, all I knew was a rough outline: that it was a town that had prospered through wholesale trade since the Edo period. But when I actually walked through the city, I encountered layers of time far richer than any textbook explanation.
