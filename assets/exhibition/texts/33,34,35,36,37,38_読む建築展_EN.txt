Season 3: Exhibition as a Medium for Architecture / 
Exhibition Caption:


Abstract Large Model / Concrete Small Model

The model placed in front of the chairs is an architectural model of the eight-story Ano Bldg. Its scale is 1/20. It is larger than us, its makers, and fits entirely within the first floor of the exhibition venue. It has an in-between scale: too small to be a real building, yet too large to be a model. The site where Ano Bldg is to stand is a parking lot facing the road on the opposite side of the exhibition venue, surrounded by buildings that have always been familiar to us.

A familiar town, familiar buildings.
A large building inside a building.
The scale that should have been remembered unconsciously appears before us, distorted.
An empty building, with no sense of human presence and no practical use. And yet, somehow nostalgic. A model with no events and no people does not provide a simulated spatial experience. Rather, together with discomfort, strangeness, and instability, it shows us the uncertainty of memory, and the richness that comes from that uncertainty. I was thinking that this might become a device that reflects not architecture itself, but rather the viewer’s mind, memory of the town, and attitude toward it.

In addition to the large model, a small 1/100 model is placed in the venue. In contrast to the 1/20 model, this model expresses details down to the materials and the forms of the sashes. The 1/100 model makes explicit the tactile and formal relationships with the six surrounding buildings. It was made as a model to express a method for constructing a façade that refers to the façades of the surrounding buildings, feels somehow nostalgic, and yet remains ambiguous in its interpretation.

The 1/20 model and the 1/100 model reverse the relationship between scale and degree of abstraction.
The 1/20 model was placed in the venue as a means of guiding a compositional reading of the architecture, while the 1/100 model was placed as a means of guiding a reading of the method.
