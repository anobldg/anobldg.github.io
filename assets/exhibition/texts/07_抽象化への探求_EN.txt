B. In Search of Abstraction

Abstraction refers to the act of extracting shared attributes from multiple objects, events, or concrete phenomena, and understanding them as a general concept. However, architecture cannot be made through such pure concepts alone.

Rather than seeking attributes shared by the objects themselves, expression may begin to depart from common sense when the maker becomes aware that what the depicted objects share is, in fact, the maker’s own way of observing and expressing them.

For example, in Paul Cézanne’s Pommes et oranges (right), the patterns and forms of different objects seem to intersect and merge with one another. This is likely because Cézanne’s own way of seeing the world is revealed there. If Cézanne had not been conscious of the act of looking itself, this expression, in which objects appear to merge, would not have emerged.

Within such expressions that depart from the expected, there are fragments of perception and interpretation that belong to the maker. There are moments when things that once appeared uniform and social begin to look complex and private. Interpretation is private rather than general, but it can become an opportunity to reconsider, in multiple ways, subjects that we tend to assume have only one meaning.

Expression that departs from the expected through interpretation is carried out by the maker, within the domain of the work, separated from the real world. Abstraction begins when the maker acknowledges that they are already inherent within the work.

If we were to redefine the shared attributes among many objects and concrete phenomena in abstraction, they would not be universal characteristics existing within the things themselves, but rather the maker’s own way of seeing the world.
